Mountainous region

American Pika The American pika is a small mammal found in the western mountains of North America. It has a round body, short limbs, and dense fur ranging from grayish-brown to reddish-brown. Pikas inhabit rocky alpine and subalpine regions, where they are active during the day and feed on grasses and herbs. They do not hibernate and instead store vegetation as hay piles for winter food. Pikas are important in their ecosystems as they influence plant communities and are prey for various predators. However, they face threats from climate change, habitat loss, and human disturbance, highlighting the need for conservation efforts to protect their alpine habitats.

Rainforest

Jaguar The jaguar is the largest big cat species in the Americas, known for its distinctive golden-yellow coat with black spots. They inhabit a variety of habitats, including rainforests and grasslands, and are solitary apex predators with a diverse diet. Jaguars are excellent swimmers and historically ranged from the southwestern United States to northern Argentina. However, habitat loss, fragmentation, and human persecution have led to their decline, and they are now listed as "Near Threatened" by the IUCN. Conservation efforts are crucial to protect their remaining populations and habitats.

Grassland

Pronghorn Antelope The pronghorn antelope is a native species of North America known for its remarkable speed and distinctive branched horns. They inhabit a variety of open habitats across western North America and are herbivores, feeding on grasses and shrubs. Pronghorns are among the fastest land animals, capable of sustained speeds up to 55 miles per hour (88 kilometers per hour). While they are not currently threatened, conservation efforts focus on habitat preservation and management to ensure their long-term survival.

Deep Sea

Giant Squid The giant squid is one of the largest invertebrates, reaching lengths up to 43 feet (13 meters). It inhabits the deep ocean, primarily in the mesopelagic and bathypelagic zones. Despite its size, the giant squid is elusive, with much of its behavior remaining a mystery. Its large eyes, up to 10 inches (25 centimeters) in diameter, are adapted for low-light conditions. Encounters with live giant squids are rare, and conservation efforts focus on protecting their deep-sea habitat from threats like pollution and climate change.
